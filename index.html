1.1. Ethernet Standards, Limits, and Frame

Ethernet Standards for Physical Layer: Ethernet has evolved over the years, with standards defining speed, media type, and distance. Key standards include: 10BASE-T (Ethernet): Runs at 10 Mbps over twisted-pair copper wire. 100BASE-TX (Fast Ethernet): Runs at 100 Mbps over twisted-pair copper wire, became the standard for many years. 1000BASE-T (Gigabit Ethernet): Runs at 1 Gbps over twisted-pair copper wire (Cat 5e or better). It is the current standard for most wired LANs. 10GBASE-T: Runs at 10 Gbps over twisted-pair copper wire (Cat 6a or better). Fiber Optic Standards (e.g., 1000BASE-SX, 10GBASE-LR): Used for longer distances and higher speeds, common in data centers and backbone networks. Ethernet Limits: The most common limit for Ethernet over twisted-pair (like 10BASE-T, 100BASE-TX, 1000BASE-T) is a maximum cable length of 100 meters per segment. This limitation is due to signal degradation and timing constraints for collision detection. Ethernet Frame: An Ethernet frame is the protocol data unit at the Data Link Layer. It encapsulates the network layer packet (e.g., an IP packet) and adds control information. The structure is as follows: Preamble & SFD (Start Frame Delimiter): Used for clock synchronization. Destination MAC Address: 6-byte address of the recipient. Source MAC Address: 6-byte address of the sender. Type / Length: Indicates the protocol of the encapsulated payload (e.g., 0x0800 for IPv4). Payload (Data): The actual data from the upper layer (46 to 1500 bytes). Frame Check Sequence (FCS): A 4-byte CRC (Cyclic Redundancy Check) value used for error detection.
